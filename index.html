Understanding Student Academic Performance

Exploring the key factors that influence student success and exam scores

Introduction

Project Overview

Student performance is influenced by a combination of academic habits, socio-economic background, parental involvement, and external environmental factors. By analyzing these diverse factors, we aim to determine which variables — such as study hours, attendance, motivation levels, and access to resources — have the greatest impact on academic success and how they interact with each other to influence student outcomes.

Our research question focuses on identifying the key factors influencing student academic performance and understanding how they impact exam scores. This analysis can provide valuable insights for educators, policymakers, and parents to design better support systems that improve student outcomes.

The analysis plan for our dataset will consist of the following tasks:

  • Data Preprocessing:
    • Handle missing values in Teacher_Quality, Parental_Education_Level, and Distance_from_Home
    • Convert categorical variables into numerical values for analysis
    • Normalize continuous features for better interpretability
  • Exploratory Data Analysis:
    • Analyze distribution of exam scores
    • Conduct correlation analysis between variables
    • Group students based on performance levels
  • Key Data Visualizations:
    • Correlation Heatmap: Visualizes the strength of relationships between numerical variables using a color-coded matrix.
    • Scatterplots (Exam Score vs Numeric Features): Explores the relationships between numeric variables such as Hours Studied, Attendance, Sleep Hours, and Previous Scores with exam outcomes.
    • Box Plots by Categorical Features: Compares the distribution of exam scores across various categorical factors such as Motivation Level, Parental Involvement, and Teacher Quality.
    • Linked Scatter & Box Plot (Interactive Visualization): Allows users to filter by study hours and exam scores to examine how sleep hours and performance vary in selected ranges.
    • Bar Chart (Family Income vs Tutoring Sessions): Shows the number of tutoring sessions grouped by family income level.
    • Parental Factors by School Type (Tableau Visualization): Uses size and color to visualize how school type, parental education level, and involvement affect average exam scores.

Data Introduction

Our analysis utilizes the Student Performance Factors Dataset from Kaggle, which contains 6,607 observations and 20 features. The dataset includes a mix of categorical variables (such as school type and gender) and continuous variables (such as study hours and exam scores). It provides comprehensive information on various personal, academic, and socio-economic factors that may influence student success. However, additional data on learning environments, teacher interaction, and psychological well-being can be better understood with extra analysis.

The attributes in our dataset include Hours_Studied, Attendance, Parental_Involvement, Access_to_Resources, Extracurricular_Activities, Sleep_Hours, Previous_Scores, Motivation_Level, Internet_Access, Tutoring_Sessions, Family_Income, Teacher_Quality, School_Type, Peer_Influence, Physical_Activity, Learning_Disabilities, Parental_Education_Level, Distance_from_Home, Gender, Exam_Score.

Visualizations

Visualization 1: Correlation Heatmap of Numerical Features

Summary and Takeaway: This is a correlation heatmap of numerical features from the student performance dataset. It visually represents how strongly each pair of numerical variables is related, using color intensity to indicate correlation values. The color bar on the right shows the scale, ranging from -1 (perfect negative correlation) to +1 (perfect positive correlation).

The correlation heatmap reveals that Attendance is the strongest predictors of exam scores, followed by Hours Studied and Previous Scores, which also shows a positive but slightly weaker correlation. While Tutoring Sessions, Sleep Hours, and Physical Activity show little to no correlation. This suggests that consistent academic habits and personal study time are key drivers of performance, whereas well-being and support factors may play a less direct role in short-term exam outcomes.

Visualization 2: Exam Score vs Numeric Feature

Summary and Takeaway: This graph is a scatter plot showing the relationship between hours studied and exam scores. Each point represents a student, with the x-axis indicating the number of hours they studied and the y-axis showing their exam score. The scatter plots reveal that hours studied has the strongest visible relationship with exam scores. As students study more, their scores generally increase, with the highest performers often studying over 25 hours. While there's still some variation, the trend suggests that increased study time contributes positively to performance. Attendance, on the other hand, shows only a slight upward trend. Most students, regardless of how often they attended, scored between 60 and 80, indicating that attendance alone doesn't strongly influence outcomes.

Sleep hours appear to have no meaningful impact on exam scores. Students across all sleep ranges—from 4 to 10 hours—achieved similar results, suggesting that while sleep may affect general well-being, it doesn't directly reflect in exam performance in this dataset. Surprisingly, previous scores also show only a mild correlation. Although one might expect strong past performance to predict future success, the plot reveals a wide variation in current exam results among students with high previous scores. This implies that while previous performance offers some context, it's not a guaranteed indicator of current outcomes, and other factors like effort, motivation, and support may play a greater role.

Visualization 3: Exam Score by Categorical Feature

Summary and Takeaway: This graph is a boxplot comparing exam scores across different levels of motivation (High, Medium and Low). Each box represents the distribution of scores for one motivation category, with the box showing the interquartile range (IQR), the line inside the box showing the median, and the dots representing outliers. Across the board, the boxplots reveal subtle but consistent trends in how different categorical factors relate to exam performance:

  • Motivation Level: Students with high or medium motivation have slightly higher average exam scores than those with low motivation. This suggests that internal drive contributes positively to performance, though the gap is not huge.
  • Parental Involvement: Higher parental involvement is associated with better exam scores. Students with involved parents scored roughly 1.7 points higher on average compared to those with low involvement, indicating that support at home plays a measurable role.
  • Access to Resources and Internet Access: These features tend to align with higher scores, reinforcing the idea that access to educational tools and online learning opportunities provides an academic advantage.
  • Teacher Quality: Students taught by teachers rated as "High" quality consistently perform better. This reflects the importance of effective instruction and teaching support in driving academic success.
  • Family Income and Parental Education Level: Students from higher-income households and families with more educated parents generally perform better, suggesting that socioeconomic background and educational culture at home matter.
  • Extracurricular Activities: Those involved in extracurriculars often score slightly higher, possibly due to better time management, discipline, or mental balance.
  • Learning Disabilities: Students with identified learning disabilities have lower average scores, highlighting the need for enhanced academic support structures.
  • Peer Influence and School Type: Positive peer influence and attending schools with better reputations (like private or well-resourced public schools) are modestly linked to higher scores.
  • Gender and Distance from Home: These show minimal differences, suggesting that performance is more shaped by academic, social, and support-based factors than demographic or logistical ones.

Visualization 4: Linked Scatter & Box Plot

Summary and Takeaway: This graph demonstrates the difference between Hours Studied, Exam Scores, and Sleep Hours. On the graph to the left, you can select a range of hours studied and a range of exam scores. Visualization 6: Parental Factors by School Type (Tableau Visualization)

Summary and Takeaway: This visualization demonstrates Parental Education Level, Parental Involvement, and Average of Exam Score broken down by School Type. The color represents Parental Education Level and Parental Involvement. Size shows the average of the Exam Score. No matter what school type or parental education level, students with low parental involvement get lower exam scores than students with medium or high parental involvement. Additionally, students whose parents receive a higher level of education seem to do better than students whose parents received a lower level of education.

Summary

What Did We Learn?

We initially thought that a lot of different variables that could not be easily changed like family income would affect the exam scores, however after looking at our visualizations it seems that we were wrong. It seems like for the most part the different factors don't affect exam performances at all, with the exception of hours studied and attendance.

Additionally, across different school types and parental education levels, children whose parents are more involved with their education outperform their peers. Overall it seems like the easiest way to boost exam scores is to attend class and increase the amount of hours spent studying (in moderation).

Future Work

For future work, this dataset offers a rich opportunity to dive deeper into predictive modeling and behavioral insights. One valuable direction would be to build a machine learning model—such as a random forest or gradient boosting regressor—to predict exam scores based on both categorical and numerical features. This could help identify non-obvious patterns and rank feature importance more precisely. Additionally, clustering techniques could be used to group students by performance profiles, revealing distinct learning personas (e.g., high-effort/high-score vs. low-effort/high-score students).

Another area to explore is interaction effects—for example, how motivation level modifies the impact of study hours or tutoring. Finally, time-based data, if available in future versions, could help understand trends in improvement or decline over multiple assessments, offering more personalized insights for educators and policy-makers.
